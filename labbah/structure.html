Understanding Sentence Structure for TOEFL

This section will guide you through the essential concepts related to sentence structure. These are crucial for improving your grammar skills in the TOEFL exam. Review each section to enhance your knowledge and apply it effectively during the test.

1. Types of Sentences

  • Declarative Sentences - Makes a statement (e.g., "The sky is blue.")
  • Interrogative Sentences - Asks a question (e.g., "Are you coming to the party?")
  • Imperative Sentences - Gives a command or request (e.g., "Please pass the salt.")
  • Exclamatory Sentences - Expresses strong feelings (e.g., "What a beautiful sunset!")

Understanding these sentence types is important for identifying sentence patterns in TOEFL grammar questions.

2. Sentence Structure

  • Simple Sentences - Contains a subject and a predicate (e.g., "She runs.")
  • Compound Sentences - Contains two independent clauses joined by a conjunction (e.g., "I like reading, and I enjoy writing.")
  • Complex Sentences - Contains an independent clause and at least one dependent clause (e.g., "Although it was raining, she went for a walk.")
  • Compound-Complex Sentences - Contains two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause (e.g., "She was tired because she worked all day, but she still went to the gym.")

In TOEFL Writing, it's essential to use varied sentence structures to demonstrate your grammatical range.

3. Subject-Verb Agreement

The subject and verb must agree in number (singular or plural). Here are some examples:

  • Singular Subject: "The dog barks loudly."
  • Plural Subject: "The dogs bark loudly."
  • Compound Subject: "John and Mary are coming to the party."

In TOEFL grammar questions, identifying subject-verb agreement correctly is crucial to avoid mistakes.

4. Common Sentence Errors

Here are some common errors to watch for in sentence structure:

  • Run-on Sentences - Two or more independent clauses are improperly joined without a conjunction or punctuation.
  • Sentence Fragments - A sentence that is incomplete or does not express a complete thought.
  • Comma Splices - Two independent clauses are joined incorrectly with just a comma.

You will likely encounter these types of errors in TOEFL grammar exercises, so make sure to practice identifying and correcting them.

5. Practice Exercises

Now, let's practice some exercises to reinforce your understanding of sentence structure. Identify the type of sentence for each example below:

  • "She likes coffee, but he prefers tea." – What type of sentence is this?
  • "Because it was cold outside, they decided to stay home." – What type of sentence is this?
  • "John and I are going to the movies." – What type of sentence is this?
  • "What a wonderful day!" – What type of sentence is this?

  • 2. Complex Sentence
